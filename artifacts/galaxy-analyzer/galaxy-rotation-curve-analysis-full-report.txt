PART III: THE EQUATION
================================================================================

STARTING POINT
--------------
Two measurable accelerations at every point in every galaxy:
  g_bar = GM_bar(r) / r²    (baryonic prediction)
  g_obs = V²(r) / r          (observed from rotation curve)

CORE EQUATION (MOND-like Transition Form)
----------------------------------------
  g_obs² = g_bar² + a₀² × ν(g_bar/a₀)

where ν(x) = x/(1+x) is a smooth interpolation function.

EXPANDED FORM
-------------
  g_obs = √(g_bar² + a₀² × g_bar / (g_bar + a₀))

VELOCITY FORM (for fitting rotation curves)
--------------------------------------------
  V(r) = √(g_obs × r)
       = √(r × √((GM/r²)² + a₀² × (GM/r²) / (GM/r² + a₀)))

COMPLETE EQUATION (No Extra Tuning Parameters)
------------------------------------------------
  g_obs = √(g_bar² + (cH₀/2π)² × g_bar / (g_bar + cH₀/2π))

Given fixed baryonic mass-to-light assumptions (Υ_disk = 0.5, Υ_bulge = 0.7)
and standard cosmological inputs (c, H₀), the equation adds no extra
galaxy-by-galaxy tuning parameters. Every quantity is either measured
locally (g_bar, which depends on adopted Υ and distance) or fixed by
cosmology (c, H₀).

NOTE: The equation is not "parameter-free" in the absolute sense. It
inherits systematic dependence on Υ assumptions, distance calibrations,
and gas mass modeling. What it avoids is any per-galaxy free parameter
analogous to a halo concentration or core radius.

PART XIII: THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK (k → Density Profile)
================================================================================

Deriving the physical meaning of k from first principles.
Why does the universe impose k ≈ V²/R?

DERIVATION: FROM k TO DARK MATTER DENSITY

Step 1: Start with empirical model
  v² = GM/r + kr
  First term = standard Newtonian gravity
  Second term (kr) grows linearly with radius

Step 2: Extract dark matter acceleration
  a_dark = v²_dark/r = k (constant!)
  A constant acceleration field independent of radius

Step 3: Derive enclosed dark mass
  M_dark(r) = k·r² / G
  Dark mass grows as r²

Step 4: Derive density profile ρ(r)
  From dM/dr = 2kr/G and ρ = (dM/dr) / (4πr²):
  ρ(r) = k / (2πGr)
  Dark matter density drops as 1/r — the NFW CUSP profile!

Step 5: Density amplitude A
  A = k / (2πG), so ρ(r) = A/r
  k = 2πGA → k is directly proportional to central density amplitude

Step 6: Empirical scaling k ≈ V²/R
  At R_max, the dark contribution v²_dark = kR sets the velocity scale.

Based on analysis of 175 SPARC galaxies (+ 22 LITTLE THINGS dwarfs),
4,123 rotation-curve measurements, using an empirical relation with no
extra galaxy-by-galaxy tuning parameters beyond fixed baryonic inputs:

The data strongly supports the existence of a characteristic acceleration
scale a₀ in galaxy dynamics that shows no significant dependence on any
tested galaxy property. This scale matches cH₀/2π within ~10%.
These are robust empirical regularities.

The apparent dark matter fraction is strongly anti-correlated with baryonic
surface density (nominal combined significance: 5.1σ over ΛCDM expectations,

is consistent with all seven systematic robustness checks.

  1. High-redshift rotation curves (JWST/ALMA)
  2. Galaxy cluster deep analysis
  3. Wide binary observations (Gaia DR4)
  4. Theoretical mechanism

The equation:
  g_obs = √(g_bar² + (cH₀/2π)² × g_bar/(g_bar + cH₀/2π))

PART XXV: MEASUREMENT PIPELINE — HOW TO EXTRACT THE SIGNAL
================================================================================

"We don't make the signal bigger — we make the noise smaller
until the signal shows through."

The 15.7% V_flat shift at z=1 is buried in ~12-35% per-galaxy noise.
Six measurement strategies were simulated (500 Monte Carlo trials each)
to determine which approach can extract the signal.

Simulation parameters:
  σ(V)/V = 12%, σ(log M) = 0.35 dex, σ(inc) = 8°
  z_low = 0, z_high = 1, N_MC = 500

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6 METHODS TESTED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  1. NAIVE: Compare mean V_flat(z=0) vs V_flat(z=1)
     → 20% detection at N=20, 58% at N=100
     Poor: mass distribution mismatch dominates

  2. MASS-MATCHED BTFR: Bin by mass, compare V within each bin
     → 93% detection at N=20, 100% at N=50
     ★ WINNER: mass matching removes the dominant systematic

  3. RATIO V_obs/V_bar: Use the ratio instead of absolute V
     → 16% detection at N=20, 55% at N=100
     Disappointing: ratio cancels some systematics but not mass scatter

  4. GLOBAL a₀ FIT: Fit a₀ directly to all galaxies at z=1
     → Significant bias (−37%): mass errors propagate into a₀
     Not recommended as primary method

  5. STACKED DIFFERENTIAL: Compare z-bins, fit trend with z
     → 72% positive trend at N=20, 86% at N=100
     Good as consistency check, not as primary detection

  6. OPTIMAL COMBINED: Ratio + mass matching + global fit + multi-z
     → The combination is not yet properly calibrated
     Needs more work on the bias correction

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
KEY RESULT: DETECTION POWER COMPARISON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Method                   N=20    N=50    N=100
  Naive V_flat             20%     33%     58%
  ★ Mass-matched BTFR      93%     99%     100%
  Ratio V/V_bar            16%     35%     55%
  Global a₀ fit            30%     22%     14%
  Stacked differential     72%     78%     86%
  Optimal combined         17%      7%      2%

THE MASS-MATCHED BTFR IS OVERWHELMINGLY SUPERIOR.

Why? Because mass uncertainty is the dominant noise source at high z.
When you bin galaxies by mass and compare V within each bin:
  - Mass scatter is controlled (same mass → same bin)
  - The V shift becomes the only variable
  - √N stacking reduces random V errors

With just 20 galaxies matched in mass bins: 93% detection rate.

PART XXVI-C: THE THREE PATHS
================================================================================

  Three derivation paths, evaluated against the 6 conditions (C1–C6).

  ──── PATH 1: de Sitter / Horizon ────

  Core argument:
    In de Sitter space, there is a cosmological horizon at R = c/H.
    This horizon has a surface gravity κ_dS = c × H_∞ = c²√(Λ/3).
    [CORRECTED] √(Λ/3) is in m⁻¹; multiplied by c gives H_∞ in s⁻¹ (a rate).
    The surface gravity (acceleration) is κ_dS = c × H_∞ = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ m/s².

  What it explains:
    - Global scale: a₀ comes from cosmology, not galaxy properties
    - Universality: same Λ everywhere → same a₀ everywhere
    - Newton limit: κ_dS only matters when g_bar → 0

  What it fails:
    - Numerical match: κ_dS = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ = 4.6× a₀ (too LARGE, not too small)
      With 2π: κ_dS/2π = 8.7×10⁻¹¹ = 0.72× a₀ (closer but still off)
    - 2π: requires an additional 2π factor to approach a₀
    - Mechanism: surface gravity of a horizon is not the same as
      modifying dynamics inside a galaxy. How does the horizon "reach in"?
    - Clusters: no prediction for the ~52.5% residual
    - Redshift: predicts constant a₀ (Λ-model), not H-evolving

  Open questions specific to this path:
    - κ_dS/2π gives ratio 0.72 — is the remaining factor 1.4 fixable?
    - Can horizon thermodynamics provide the mechanism?

  ──── PATH 2: Unruh / Thermodynamic ────

  Core argument:
    A uniformly accelerated observer sees thermal radiation at
    T_U = ℏa/(2πck_B). The universe itself has a de Sitter temperature
    T_dS = ℏc√(Λ/3)/(2πk_B). When a particle's acceleration is so low
    that T_U(a) < T_dS, the Unruh effect can no longer distinguish
    the particle's acceleration from the cosmic background. Below this
    threshold, inertia (= resistance to acceleration) is modified.

    Threshold: T_U(a_min) = T_GH → a_min = c × H_∞ = κ_dS = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ m/s²
    [CORRECTED] The 2π in T_U cancels with the 2π in T_GH (see Part XXVII).

    This is the Milgrom-Unruh connection (see also: Verlinde 2016,
    McCulloch 2007, Smolin 2017).

  What it explains:
    - WHY a₀ appears: it is the boundary where local and cosmic
      thermal scales merge
    - Universality: cosmic temperature is the same everywhere
    - Newton limit: T_U >> T_GH means acceleration is "sharp"
      and inertia is standard Newtonian

  What it fails:
    - 2π cancellation: T_U = T_GH gives a = cH_∞ (the 2π cancels out),
      so κ_dS = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ = 4.6× a₀ — too large, not too small
    - Mechanism inside galaxies: Unruh radiation is a QFT result for
      uniformly accelerated observers in flat space. Real galaxy
      dynamics involve non-uniform, curved spacetime. The translation
      from "Unruh threshold" to "rotation curve modification" is not
      established.
    - Clusters: no specific prediction
    - If we use H₀ instead of √(Λ/3) in the Unruh argument:
      T_H = ℏH₀/(2πk_B) gives a_min = cH₀ (no 2π in a_min).
      Then a₀ = cH₀, not cH₀/2π. Off by factor 2π in the
      opposite direction. The 2π arithmetic is subtle here.

  Open questions specific to this path:
    - Can the Unruh argument be made rigorous in curved spacetime?
    - Which temperature (T_dS or T_H = ℏH₀/2πk_B) is the right
      cosmic reference? They differ by factor H₀/H_∞ ≈ 1.23.
    - Is modified inertia the right physical picture, or is it
      modified gravity? (They are observationally different.)

  ──── PATH 3: Dark-Sector Coupling ────

  Core argument:
    Dark matter exists (as in ΛCDM), but its distribution is not
    independent of baryons. A coupling mechanism — gravitational
    feedback, self-interaction, phase transition (superfluid DM),
    or dark photon mediation — forces DM halos to track baryon
    distributions below a critical acceleration.

    The scale a₀ emerges from the coupling physics, not from
    cosmology directly. Example: in superfluid DM (Berezhiani &
    Khoury 2015), the superfluid phonon force mimics MOND below
    a critical acceleration set by the DM particle mass and
    self-coupling.

  What it explains:
    - Clusters: DM is real, so clusters work naturally
    - Newton limit: standard gravity + DM at high density
    - Mechanism: has one (coupling/feedback/phase transition)
    - RAR tightness: potentially, if coupling is deterministic

  What it fails:
    - cH₀ coincidence: no natural reason for a₀ ~ cH₀/2π
      The coupling constant would have to be tuned.
    - Universality: coupling depends on formation history.
      Why is the scatter so small?
    - a₀ derivation: in superfluid DM, a₀ depends on DM particle
      parameters (mass, coupling). These are free parameters.
      a₀ is fitted, not derived.
    - Redshift evolution: depends on model specifics. Some predict
      evolution, some don't. Not a clean prediction.

  Open questions specific to this path:
    - Can any DM coupling model predict a₀ ≈ cH₀/2π from particle
      physics parameters alone?
    - Can feedback models reproduce RAR scatter < 0.13 dex?
    - Is superfluid DM phase transition radius the right scale
      to produce the a₀ transition?

PART XXVII: FIRST DERIVATION ATTEMPT — UNRUH / de SITTER, STEP BY STEP
================================================================================

  Goal: Start from physics, not from data. See if a₀ comes out.
  Method: Follow the logic to its conclusion. Mark exactly where it breaks.

  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  STEP 1: THE UNRUH EFFECT (established physics)
  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Result (Unruh 1976, Fulling 1973, Davies 1975):
    A uniformly accelerated observer in Minkowski vacuum sees a
    thermal bath at temperature:

      T_U = ℏa / (2πck_B)

  This is not controversial. It follows from:
    - Quantization of a scalar field in Rindler coordinates
    - The Bogoliubov transformation between Rindler and Minkowski vacua
    - The periodicity of Euclidean time: β = 2πc/a

  Key point: The 2π is NOT inserted by hand.
    It comes from the periodicity condition in imaginary time.
    The Euclidean Rindler metric has period 2πc/a in τ_E.
    The KMS (Kubo-Martin-Schwinger) condition requires β = 1/kT.
    Therefore T = ℏa/(2πck_B).

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ STATUS: The 2π has a rigorous origin in the Unruh effect.         │
  │ It is NOT a patch. It comes from Euclidean periodicity.           │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  STEP 2: THE de SITTER TEMPERATURE (established physics)
  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Result (Gibbons & Hawking 1977):
    An observer in de Sitter space (Λ > 0) sees a thermal bath at:

      T_dS = ℏH_Λ / (2πk_B)

    where H_Λ = c√(Λ/3) is the asymptotic Hubble parameter.

  This is the same structure as the Unruh effect, but the
  "acceleration" is replaced by c·H_Λ (the surface gravity
  of the cosmological horizon):

      κ_dS = c·H_Λ = c²√(Λ/3)

  Numerically:
    H_Λ = c√(Λ/3) = √(1.11×10⁻⁵² × (3×10⁸)² / 3) = 1.88×10⁻¹⁸ s⁻¹
    T_dS = ℏH_Λ/(2πk_B) = (1.055×10⁻³⁴)(1.88×10⁻¹⁸)/((6.28)(1.381×10⁻²³))
         = 2.28×10⁻³⁰ K

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ STATUS: de Sitter space has a temperature. This is established.   │
  │ The temperature is ~10⁻³⁰ K. Extremely cold, but not zero.       │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  STEP 3: THE THRESHOLD ARGUMENT (the key step)
  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Conjecture (Milgrom 1999, McCulloch 2007, Verlinde 2016 variants):

    When a particle's acceleration a is large, its Unruh temperature
    T_U(a) >> T_dS. The particle "sees" its own acceleration clearly
    against the cosmic thermal background. Inertia works normally.

    When a drops so low that T_U(a) ≈ T_dS, the Unruh bath merges
    with the cosmic background. The particle can no longer "feel" its
    own acceleration distinctly. Inertia is modified.

    The threshold is:

      T_U(a_min) = T_dS
      ℏa_min/(2πck_B) = ℏH_Λ/(2πk_B)
      a_min = c·H_Λ = c²√(Λ/3)

  Numerically:
    a_min = c²√(Λ/3) = (3×10⁸)² × √(1.11×10⁻⁵² / 3)
          = 9×10¹⁶ × 6.08×10⁻²⁷
          ≈ 5.5×10⁻¹⁰ m/s²

    Wait — let me redo this carefully:
    √(Λ/3) = √(3.7×10⁻⁵³) = 6.08×10⁻²⁷ m⁻¹
    c√(Λ/3) = 3×10⁸ × 6.08×10⁻²⁷ = 1.82×10⁻¹⁸ s⁻¹ = H_Λ ✓
    c²√(Λ/3) = 3×10⁸ × 1.82×10⁻¹⁸ = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ m/s²

    Hmm. That gives 5.5×10⁻¹⁰, not 7.07×10⁻¹¹.

    Correction — the confusion is between c√(Λ/3) and c²√(Λ/3):
    H_Λ = c√(Λ/3) has units s⁻¹? No.
    √(Λ/3) has units m⁻¹ (since Λ has units m⁻²).
    c√(Λ/3) has units m/s × m⁻¹ = s⁻¹. This is H_Λ.
    The surface gravity κ = cH_Λ = c²√(Λ/3) has units m/s².

    Let me be precise:
    Λ = 1.11×10⁻⁵² m⁻²
    √(Λ/3) = √(3.70×10⁻⁵³) = 6.08×10⁻²⁷ m⁻¹
    H_Λ = c × 6.08×10⁻²⁷ = 1.825×10⁻¹⁸ s⁻¹
    κ = c × H_Λ = 3×10⁸ × 1.825×10⁻¹⁸ = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ m/s²

    But this is κ = c²√(Λ/3) ≈ 5.5×10⁻¹⁰ m/s².
    This is ~4.6× a₀, not 0.59× a₀.

    The discrepancy from Part XXVI used a different formula.
    Let me reconcile:

    In Part XXVI: "a_dS = c√(Λ/3) = 7.065×10⁻¹¹ m/s²"
    But c√(Λ/3) has units s⁻¹, not m/s². This was H_Λ, not a_dS.
    To get acceleration: a_dS = c × H_Λ = c²√(Λ/3) = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ m/s².

    CORRECTION: The script in Part XXVI computed c√(Λ/3) with c in m/s
    and √(Λ/3) in m⁻¹, getting 1.82×10⁻¹⁸ m⁻¹·m/s = 1.82×10⁻¹⁸ s⁻¹,

    κ_dS = c²√(Λ/3) = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ m/s²

    Ratio κ_dS / a₀ = 5.47×10⁻¹⁰ / 1.2×10⁻¹⁰ = 4.6

    So de Sitter surface gravity is ~4.6× LARGER than a₀, not smaller.

  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  STEP 4: WHERE THE 2π RE-ENTERS
  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  In Step 3, the 2π cancelled because both sides had the Unruh form.
  But a₀ ≈ cH₀/2π, which means a factor of 2π IS present in the
  relationship between a₀ and cH₀.

  Where could it come from?

  The threshold gives a_threshold = cH (either H₀ or H_Λ).
  The observation gives a₀ ≈ cH₀/2π.
  The ratio is a₀/a_threshold = 1/2π ≈ 0.16.

  Possible origins of this extra 1/2π:

  Option A: The threshold is not T_U = T_dS, but T_U = T_dS/(2π)
    This would require a physical reason for the extra factor.
    No known argument provides this.

  Option B: The transition is not sharp but gradual.
    If the interpolation function is μ(x) = x/(1+x) where x = a/a₀,
    then the "effective" threshold (where μ = 0.5) is at a = a₀,
    but the onset of deviation begins at a ~ 2π × a₀.
    This reverses the logic: a₀ is the midpoint, not the onset.

  Option C: The relevant cosmic scale is ω = H/(2π), not H itself.
    If H₀ is a frequency f, the angular frequency is ω = 2πf = 2πH₀.

PART XXVIII: THE DYNAMICAL QUESTION — MODIFIED INERTIA vs MODIFIED FIELD
================================================================================

  The question has shifted:
    OLD: "Why is a₀ = cH₀/2π?"
    NEW: "Given that a₀ exists, what dynamical equation produces the RAR?"

  Part XXVII showed that Unruh/de Sitter gives the right order but not
  the exact value. The thermal derivation is suggestive but incomplete.
  We now set aside the ORIGIN of a₀ and ask: what LAW uses it?

  Two candidate dynamical frameworks:
    A. Modified Inertia (MI): the response of matter to force changes at low a
    B. Modified Field Equation (MFE): the gravitational field equation changes

  These are NOT the same physics. They make different predictions.

  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  FRAMEWORK A: MODIFIED INERTIA
  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Core idea:
    Newton's second law F = ma is exact at high acceleration.
    At low acceleration (a << a₀), the effective inertial mass changes:

      F = m · μ(a/a₀) · a

    where μ(x) → 1 when x >> 1 (Newton recovered)
    and   μ(x) → x when x << 1 (deep-MOND limit)

    Equivalently: the "felt" acceleration a_felt relates to Newtonian a_N by:

      a_N = a_felt · μ(a_felt/a₀)

    This is Milgrom's original MOND formulation (1983).

  What it predicts:
    In the deep regime (a_felt << a₀):
      a_N = a_felt · (a_felt/a₀) = a_felt²/a₀
      → a_felt = √(a_N · a₀)

    For circular orbits: a_felt = V²/r, a_N = GM/r²
      V²/r = √(GM/r² · a₀)
      V⁴ = GM · a₀
      → V_flat = (GMa₀)^(1/4)

    This IS the Baryonic Tully-Fisher Relation. It falls out directly.

  How it connects to RAR:
    The RAR is g_obs = f(g_bar) where g_bar = a_N and g_obs = a_felt.
    MI says: g_bar = g_obs · μ(g_obs/a₀)
    Solving for g_obs: g_obs = ν(g_bar/a₀) · g_bar

    With the "simple" interpolation function:
      ν(y) = [1 + √(1 + 4/y)] / 2

    This produces the exact shape of the observed RAR.

  Strengths:
    ✓ BTFR derived, not fitted
    ✓ RAR shape predicted
    ✓ Newton limit automatic
    ✓ Single parameter a₀ — universal
    ✓ Works for both HSB and LSB galaxies

  Weaknesses:
    ✗ a₀ is inserted, not derived
    ✗ The interpolation function μ(x) is not unique — many forms work
    ✗ Modified inertia is non-local in general (Milgrom 1994):
      if F = m·μ·a, then in non-circular orbits the equation depends
      on the ENTIRE trajectory, not just instantaneous state.
      This makes it hard to formulate as a local field theory.
    ✗ No relativistic completion that works fully
    ✗ Clusters: still needs ~2× more mass

  The non-locality problem (critical):
    Milgrom (1994) showed that MI applied to non-circular orbits
    requires knowledge of the orbit's future and past. This is because
    the modification depends on |a|, which for a general orbit depends
    on the path. In circular orbits this problem disappears (|a| = V²/r
    is constant). But for stars on eccentric orbits, or for systems
    without symmetry, MI becomes non-local in time.

    This is a deep theoretical problem, not a technicality.
    It means MI as stated is not a complete theory — it's a prescription
    that works for symmetric cases.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Connection to our framework:
    Our formula uses:
      g_obs² = g_bar² + a₀²·g_bar/(g_bar + a₀)

    [CORRECTED — see Part XXIX] The deep-MOND limit of this equation is:
    g_bar → 0:  g_obs² ≈ a₀·g_bar → 0,  so g_obs ≈ √(a₀·g_bar) → 0.
    This is standard deep-MOND behavior, NOT a floor.

    Our framework is CLOSER to MI than to MFE because it modifies the
    observed acceleration, not the field equation.

    It gives the SAME deep limit as standard MI:
    - MI deep limit: g_obs = √(g_bar · a₀)  → goes to 0 as g_bar → 0
    - Our framework: g_obs ≈ √(a₀ · g_bar)  → goes to 0 as g_bar → 0

    The difference is in the interpolation function, not the asymptotic limits.
    a₀ remains a transition scale, not a minimum acceleration.

  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  FRAMEWORK B: MODIFIED FIELD EQUATION (AQUAL / modified Poisson)
  ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Core idea:
    The gravitational potential Φ obeys a modified Poisson equation:

      ∇·[μ(|∇Φ|/a₀) ∇Φ] = 4πGρ

    where μ(x) → 1 for x >> 1 and μ(x) → x for x << 1.

    This is AQUAL (AQUAdratic Lagrangian), Bekenstein & Milgrom 1984.

  Key difference from MI:
    In MI, you modify the LEFT side of F = ma (the inertia).
    In MFE, you modify the gravitational FIELD itself (the force).

    For spherically symmetric systems, MI and MFE give identical results.
    For non-symmetric systems, they differ.

  The Lagrangian:
    AQUAL has a well-defined action:

      S = -∫ d³x [a₀²/(8πG)] F(|∇Φ|²/a₀²) + ∫ d³x ρΦ

    where F'(s) = μ(√s).

    This is a genuine field theory with:
    - A Lagrangian
    - Euler-Lagrange equations
    - Conservation laws
    - Local dynamics (no non-locality problem)

  What it predicts:
    For spherical symmetry: same as MI (BTFR, RAR, rotation curves).
    For non-spherical: different from MI.

    Key prediction unique to MFE:
      The External Field Effect (EFE).
      A galaxy falling in an external field g_ext modifies the
      INTERNAL dynamics even if g_ext is uniform. This violates
      the Strong Equivalence Principle.

      In MI: a uniform external field can be transformed away (SEP holds).
      In MFE: it cannot (SEP violated).

    EFE has been claimed detected in some dwarf galaxies
    (Chae et al. 2020, but debated — Freundlich et al. 2022).

  Strengths:
    ✓ BTFR and RAR derived (same as MI for spherical)
    ✓ Has a Lagrangian — proper field theory
    ✓ Local dynamics — no non-locality problem
    ✓ Makes a unique prediction (EFE) testable in principle
    ✓ Newton limit automatic

  Weaknesses:
    ✗ a₀ is still inserted as a parameter
    ✗ μ(x) is not unique
    ✗ No satisfactory relativistic extension (TeVeS has problems)
    ✗ Clusters: same problem as MI
    ✗ The Lagrangian F(s) has no deeper derivation

    N = 175 galaxies with resolved rotation curves
    For each galaxy: M_bar (stellar + gas), V_flat (flat-regime velocity)
    Fit local BTFR: log V_flat = (1/4) log M_bar + C₀
    Record normalization C₀ with bootstrap confidence interval
    Divide into 4 mass bins:
      Bin 1: log M_bar = 8.5-9.5 (dwarfs)
      Bin 2: log M_bar = 9.5-10.0 (intermediate)
      Bin 3: log M_bar = 10.0-10.5 (massive)
      Bin 4: log M_bar = 10.5-11.5 (giant spirals)

  STEP 2: HIGH-z SAMPLE SELECTION
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Source: JWST NIRSpec IFU + ALMA CO/[CII] kinematics
    Target: 60-80 galaxies at z = 0.5-1.0
    Requirements:
      (a) Spatially resolved rotation curves (≥ 5 independent points)
      (b) Baryonic mass from SED fitting (stellar) + CO/[CII] (gas)
      (c) Inclination > 30° (to avoid sin(i) errors)
      (d) V/σ > 2 (rotation-dominated, not dispersion-dominated)
      (e) No major mergers or strong bars
    Mass matching: select high-z galaxies to populate same 4 mass bins
    with ≥ 15-20 per bin.

  STEP 3: SYSTEMATIC BUDGET
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Per-galaxy uncertainties:
      - V_flat:  ±10-15% (beam smearing, inclination, pressure support)
      - M_bar:   ±0.2 dex (SED fitting, IMF, gas fraction)
      - Distance: ±5% (from spectroscopic redshift + cosmology)

    Systematic floors:
      - IMF evolution: could shift M_bar by ±0.1 dex at z=1
      - Gas fraction: higher at z=1, may bias M_bar if not properly measured
      - Beam smearing: systematically LOWERS V_flat → biases AGAINST our signal
        (conservative: if we detect +14%, it's not beam smearing)
      - Selection effects: star-forming galaxies preferentially selected at high z

    Mitigation:
      - Mass matching eliminates mass-dependent systematics
      - Bootstrap resampling accounts for per-galaxy scatter
      - Beam smearing correction using forward-modeled kinematic fits
      - Multiple gas tracers (CO for z<1, [CII] for z>1) for cross-check

  STEP 4: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Primary test:
      Fit R(z) = E(z)^α across mass-matched bins
      H₀ (null): α = 0 (no evolution → MOND / Λ-based)
      H₁ (alternative): α = 1 (evolution → H-based scale)

    Test statistic:
      Likelihood ratio: Λ = -2 ln(L(α=0) / L(α=α_best))
      Distributed as χ²(1) under the null

    Power analysis (pre-registration):
      Signal: α = 1 → ΔR = 0.70 at z=1
      Noise per bin: σ(R) ≈ 4σ(V)/V × √(1/N_bin) ≈ 0.10 at N=20
      SNR = 0.70 / 0.10 = 7.0 → detection power > 99% at 3σ threshold

      At z = 0.5: signal = 0.28, noise = 0.10 → SNR = 2.8 → power ~80%
      At z = 1.0: signal = 0.70, noise = 0.10 → SNR = 7.0 → power ~99%

    Minimum viable experiment:
      20 galaxies per mass bin × 4 bins = 80 galaxies at z = 0.5-1.0
      This gives 3σ detection power of 93% at z=1 (conservative estimate)

  STEP 5: DECISION TREE
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    RESULT: α = 1.0 ± 0.15
      → Cosmic Scale model CONFIRMED
      → a₀ tracks H(z), cosmological origin strongly supported
      → Standard MOND falsified (a₀ is not a fundamental constant)
      → Next: derive the mechanism linking H to local dynamics

    RESULT: α = 0.0 ± 0.15
      → Cosmic Scale model FALSIFIED
      → a₀ does not evolve with z
      → MOND or Λ-based origin supported
      → cH₀/2π is a numerical coincidence (or Λ-based, which is constant)

    RESULT: α = 0.5 ± 0.3 (ambiguous)
      → Need more data (increase N or extend to z > 1)
      → Or: a₀ evolves more weakly than E(z)
      → Consider H(z)^β model and fit β

    RESULT: scatter increases dramatically at high z
      → ΛCDM feedback model may be correct
      → a₀ is emergent from galaxy formation, not fundamental
      → No clean BTFR exists at high z
